March 2026 Is a Gaming Avalanche — How to Actually Keep Track (And Not Miss Anything)

Bungie, Blizzard, Pearl Abyss, and Mega Crit all decided to drop their biggest games in the same month. March 2026 is one of the most stacked release schedules in recent memory. Here's how I'm surviving it without losing my mind — or my backlog.

I don't know who at these major studios got together and said "let's all release our biggest games in March 2026," but here we are.

We've got:

  • World of Warcraft: Midnight — March 2
  • Marathon (Bungie's first new IP since Halo) — March 5
  • Slay the Spire 2 (the sequel we waited years for) — March 5 Early Access
  • Crimson Desert (Pearl Abyss's ambitious open-world RPG) — March 19

And that's just the headlines. There are at least 15 other notable releases this month that I care about. My wallet is already crying.

The real problem isn't the money though. It's the tracking. How do you keep all of these straight? How do you remember which ones you pre-ordered, which ones you're waiting for reviews on, which ones you need to clear time for? How do you make sure you don't accidentally buy something twice or forget you already own it?

Last gaming season, I missed a release I'd been excited about for months because it just... disappeared from my mental radar. I randomly stumbled on it three weeks after launch and felt like an idiot. Never again.

The "Just Use a Calendar" Lie

Everyone's first suggestion: "Just put them in your calendar!"

Okay, sure. But a calendar isn't enough. What about ones you're on the fence about? What about games you want to wait for reviews? What about tracking prices? What about remembering which studio is making it, what platform it's on, whether it has co-op?

Calendars only handle dates. We need more context than that.

🔥

The average serious gamer is tracking 20-40 upcoming titles across multiple platforms at any given time. That's not counting their existing backlog of games they already own but haven't finished.

What Actually Works: A Release Tracker Database

After drowning in spreadsheets and sticky notes, I finally landed on a simple Notion database that does the job without overcomplicating things.

Here's what fields I actually use:

Essential Fields

  • Title — Obvious. But having it as the title property means you can search instantly.
  • Release Date — Date property. I sort by this to see what's coming up next.
  • Status — Select: "Tracking," "Pre-ordered," "Waiting for Reviews," "Maybe later," "Not interested."
  • Platform — Multi-select: PC, PS5, Xbox Series X|S, Switch, etc.
  • Genre — Helps me filter by what mood I'm in. "I want to play an RPG" should be a filter that works.

Helpful But Not Critical

  • Studio/Developer — Good for tracking studios you trust (or avoid).
  • Store Link — URL property so you can jump straight to the store page.
  • Notes — Why you're excited, co-op info, anticipated playtime, whatever matters to you.
  • Wishlist Price — If you're waiting for a sale, put your target price here.

That's it. Nothing fancy. The power comes from actually using it — checking it weekly, updating statuses when you decide to pre-order or pass, and most importantly, sorting by release date so you always know what's next.

Views That Actually Help

Once you've got the database, create these views (I'm telling you — this works):

  • "Upcoming This Month" — Filter: Release date is within current month. Shows you what you need to prepare for.
  • "Pre-ordered" — Filter: Status = Pre-ordered. Just to confirm you actually pre-ordered (and didn't imagine it).
  • "Waiting for Reviews" — Filter: Status = Waiting for Reviews. Review this list 3-4 days after launch and update statuses.
  • "Maybe Later" — The graveyard of hopeful curiosity. Check this every 3 months — you'll be surprised how many things you no longer care about.
💡

The goal isn't to track everything. It's to track what matters to you. If you don't care about sports games, don't track them. Be selective.
