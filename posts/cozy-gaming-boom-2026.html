The Cozy Gaming Boom of 2026: How to Actually Keep Up

2026 is the year of cozy games. Witchbrook, Tales of Seikyu, Loftia, My Time at Evershine — they're all coming. If you're like me, your wishlist is already 50 games deep and growing. Here's how I'm using Notion to stay sane and actually play the ones I care about.

Every time I open Reddit or YouTube this month, it's "Witchbrook this" and "Tales of Seikyu that." The cozy game pipeline is packed. 2026 might be the biggest year for relaxing games ever.

And honestly? I'm thrilled. I love my Souls-likes as much as the next masochist, but sometimes you just want to farm some crops and befriend a town full of weirdos without the threat of instant death around every corner.

But there's a problem. When every game feels chill and approachable, everything starts looking equally appealing. Your Steam wishlist explodes. Your "to-play" list becomes a psychological burden. You end up scrolling through store pages for an hour, paralyzed by choice, and then play nothing at all.

I've been there. I still am there, honestly. But I've built a Notion system that's actually helping. And if you're drowning in cozy game FOMO, maybe it'll help you too.

Why Cozy Games Are Different

Cozy games don't have that "must-play NOW" urgency that blockbuster AAA titles have. There's no cultural wave to ride — you can play Witchbrook three months after launch and miss nothing. That's the whole point.

But that freedom is also a trap. Without deadlines, without hype cycles, cozy games just... sit there. They accumulate. They become good intentions. They're the "I'll start playing this weekend" game that weekend never comes for.

The solution isn't to play more. It's to decide, intentionally, what you actually want to play — and stop pretending you'll get to everything.

My Notion Setup for Cozy Games

I call it the Cozy Queue. It's a simple Notion database with a few key views that match my different moods.

1. The "Right Now" View

This is my default. It shows games that are: (a) released, (b) not yet completed, and (c) marked as "current priority." I keep only 2–3 items in this view at any time. When I finish one, I pick the next from the backlog and promote it.

The magic is in limiting the visible options. Choice Paralysis™ is defeated by having too few choices, not too many.

2. The "Coming Soon" Tracker

Release dates matter for cozy games more than you think. You don't want to be playing My Time at Evershine two weeks before Loftia drops — that's how you bounce off both.

I track upcoming releases with expected dates, and I have a view that shows me everything launching in the next 90 days. That way I can plan ahead: maybe I finish my current game a week before something big drops, so I have buffer time.

3. The Mood-Based Filter

Sometimes I want farming, sometimes I want crafting, sometimes I want exploration, sometimes I want story. I tag each game with its primary vibe and have saved filters for each mood.

Feeling stressed? Filter by "chill" or "comfort." Bored? Filter by "exploration" or "progression." It's like Netflix categories but for games I actually own.

4. The Playtime Tracker

Cozy games can easily eat 100+ hours. I track how long I've played each one (Notion has a number property) and set personal benchmarks. "I want to finish this before hitting 50 hours" is oddly motivating — it keeps me moving instead of just meandering forever.

🌱

The goal isn't to rush through cozy games. It's to play intentionally so you actually experience the games you love instead of watching them gather digital dust.

The Real Secret: It's Not About the System

I could give you my exact Notion template and it might not work for you. Because the system only works if you actually open it.

Here's what I've learned about habit formation:

  • Make it stupid easy. I have a button in my phone's home screen that opens my Cozy Queue directly. One tap.
  • Review weekly. Every Sunday I spend 5 minutes scanning the tracker, adjusting priorities, deleting games I know I'll never play.
  • No judgment. Some games I'll never finish. That's fine. I mark them as "dropped" and move on. Guilt is the enemy of fun.

The point is to have a single source of truth. Not scattered Steam tags, not wishlists across three different stores, not Post-it notes on your monitor. One place that tells you what's next.

Where to Find the Games

While building this, I realized there's no single page that tracks all the cozy releases coming up. So I added a "Discovery" section to my tracker — links to Steam,itch.io, and various cozy gaming subreddits I check monthly.

If you're looking for new releases, I recommend following:

  • r/CozyGamers — the daily threads are gold
  • Cozy Games List on Steam (curated community list)
  • YouTube channels like Cozy Gaming and the Cozy Corner

And of course, keep an eye on the major 2026 releases: Witchbrook, Tales of Seikyu, My Time at Evershine, Loftia, Fields of Mistria. They're going to dominate the space.

⚠️

Cozy game wishlists have a way of becoming "I'll play this someday" tombs. If a game's been on your list for more than 6 months and you haven't even checked a trailer recently, maybe it's not meant to be. Give yourself permission to remove it.
